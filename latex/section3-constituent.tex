
\section{Constituents in a the verbal construction in UD}\label{UD}

In this paper, we suppose a certain knowledge of the UD annotation scheme. We just recall that the syntactic structure of all sentences is encoded by a dependency tree. Here we focus on verbal constructions, that is a verb and all the constituents that depend on it.  Note that we decide to consider all lexical verbs and to not restrain our study to the main verbs (see (\ref{ex:notmain})) for at least two reasons: first it can double or triple the number of constructions and this is not insignificant when the corpus is not big; second, in some sentences, the most interesting verb, in terms of construction, can be subordinated because the main verb is only a modal verb for instance.

Not all units depending on a verb can be considered as constituents in the verbal construction. We thus eliminated punctuation signs (which in UD is attached by a relation \texttt{punct}), discourse markers (relation \texttt{discourse}) (see \textit{alas} in (\ref{ex:aux})), juxtaposed clauses or parentheses (relation \texttt{parataxis}), conjuncts in a coordination (relation \texttt{conj}) (\ref{ex:coord}), or coordinating conjunctions (relation \texttt{cc}) (see \textit{but} in (\ref{ex:aux})). We excluded the \texttt{vocative} relation, which concerns addresses to a participant (\textit{Guys, take it easy!}), but kept dislocated arguments (\textit{These guys, I don’t trust them.}), especially because in some languages the line between governed units and dislocated units is difficult to draw. We also exclude the \texttt{aux} relation (see \textit{do} in (\ref{ex:aux})), considering that auxiliaries are part of the verb form, as well as compound (see \textit{out} in \ref{ex:out}). The markers of the subordination such as subordinating conjunctions and adpositions (relations \texttt{mark} and \texttt{case}), which are analyzed as dependents in UD but are generally considered as heads of the constructions, are also discarded. 

Some examples of verbal constructions in English as considered in this study are given in (\ref{ex:1}); the verb is underlined and the constituents are shown between square brackets; elements that are filtered are given in italics.

\pex\label{ex:1}
\a\label{ex:aux}\textit{But} [I] , \textit{alas} , \textit{do} [not] \underline{know} [how to see sheep] [through the walls of boxes]. \corpus{en\_littleprince}
\a\label{ex:notmain}This means that \textit{when} [you] \underline{move} [a series or category field] [to the filter area and back], previously hidden items are again hidden. \corpus{en\_lines}
\a\label{ex:coord}[You] \underline{tell} [me] [what they look like] \textit{and I'll tell you what they are}. \corpus{en\_childes}
\a\label{ex:out}[One minister] [reportedly] \underline{handed} \textit{out} [100 dollar 'gifts'] [to journalists attending a press conference for Allawi], [a practice that brings back bad memories to many Iraqis]. \corpus{en\_ewt}
\xe

We also took into account the fact that a unit can be broken in two continuous parts in case of extraction or extraposition and decided to include these cases as two separate constituents. For instance, in (\ref{ex:what}), the relative pronoun \textit{what} is extracted and separated from its governor \textit{do}, and, in (\ref{ex:extra}), the relative clause \textit{that go well with the Santorum cocktail} is extraposed and separated from its governor \textit{dishes}.

\pex
\a\label{ex:what}[What] \textit{do} [you] \underline{like} [to do] ? \corpus{en\_ewt} 
\a\label{ex:extra}[What culinary dishes] \textit{would} [you] \underline{recommend} [that go well with the Santorum cocktail]? \corpus{en\_gum}
\xe

For each language L and each $n \geq$ 1, we extracted all the verbal constructions with $n$ constituents. We then calculated the size of each constituent, punctations excluded. In other words, we kept all the relations except \texttt{punct} and if the constituent contains a coordination or a discourse marker there is no reason to remove them. We call MAL$_n$(L) the mean size of the constituents in all the verbal constructions with $n$ constituents. Note that we compute MAL$_n$(L) in a given language L only when we have at least 100 constructions with $n$ constituents. 

We define LMAL$_n$(L) and RMAL$_n$(L) in the same way. For LMAL, we select all the constructions with $n$ preverbal constituents (i.e.\ on the left), regardless on the other size of the verb. Same thing for RMAL with all the constructions with $n$ postverbal constituents (i.e.\ on the right).
